# 1. Introduction

One Mandaluyong Super App is a unified, AI-assisted, citizen-centric digital platform designed to modernize the delivery of public services and strengthen the operational capabilities of the City Government of Mandaluyong.

Rather than functioning as a standalone mobile application, the platform serves as an integrated digital ecosystem that brings together government services, departments, information, and operational processes into a single, secure, and interoperable platform.

The project adopts a solution-oriented engineering approach, recognizing that digital transformation extends beyond simply digitizing existing processes. Instead, it focuses on understanding real-world challenges, engineering sustainable solutions, improving interoperability, preserving institutional knowledge, and continuously enhancing government capabilities over time.

One Mandaluyong Super App is designed to complement and strengthen the City's existing digital initiatives, including its official website and online services, by providing a modern digital experience for citizens while improving internal government coordination, operational efficiency, engineering governance, and evidence-based decision support.

Artificial intelligence within the platform is designed to augment—not replace—human expertise. AI serves as an engineering and decision-support capability that assists government leaders, engineers, architects, analysts, project teams, and employees by providing evidence-based insights, validation, recommendations, knowledge reuse, and continuous organizational learning while preserving human authority, accountability, and governance.

The platform is envisioned as a long-term digital foundation capable of evolving alongside the changing needs of Mandaluyong City, supporting future innovations, interoperability with appropriate national government platforms, and continuous improvement through engineering excellence.

# 7. Problem Statement

The City Government of Mandaluyong has made significant progress in providing digital information and online services through its official website and various departmental initiatives. However, as with many growing digital government environments, citizens and government personnel may still encounter challenges arising from fragmented user experiences, disconnected systems, duplicated information, manual processes, and inconsistent access to digital services.

Citizens often need to navigate multiple pages, processes, offices, or communication channels to complete a single government transaction. At the same time, departments may operate independent information systems and data repositories that, while serving their individual operational requirements, can make interoperability, collaboration, reporting, and long-term knowledge management more complex.

As digital services continue to expand, there is an increasing need for a unified digital platform that simplifies citizen engagement while strengthening internal government operations, engineering governance, data management, interoperability, institutional learning, and evidence-based decision-making.

The objective is not to replace existing systems or digital initiatives, but to provide a solution-oriented platform that integrates, enhances, and continuously improves the City's digital ecosystem while preserving previous investments and supporting future innovation.

# 11. IT Governance Structure

Section 10 identifies who is *involved* in One Mandaluyong Super App. This section defines who *decides* — the governance bodies responsible for approving architecture, budget, and direction, so that the architecture-review and approval steps already assumed throughout the Development Workflow, Branching Strategy, and Risk Management sections have an actual body behind them.

> ⚠️ **Partly Proposed, Partly Now a Legal Requirement**
> The governance bodies below reflect common practice for LGU digital transformation initiatives, informed by the offices already confirmed to exist in Mandaluyong's own published department structure — these remain proposals pending confirmation by the **City Administrator's Department**, using the same proposed-until-confirmed approach already applied to the Risk Register's ownership assignments.
>
> However, the **Chief Information Officer position below is no longer merely a proposal.** Republic Act No. 12254 (the E-Governance Act of 2025) requires all covered government agencies, including LGUs, to designate a Chief Information Officer to lead digital transformation and ensure alignment with national ICT standards, and requires agency ICT strategies to align with DICT's E-Government Master Plan (EGMP). This section should be read as identifying a legal obligation the City must fulfill, not just a best-practice recommendation.
>
> **Statutory deadlines, corrected:** RA 12254 was signed September 5, 2025 and took effect 15 days after publication (approximately late September 2025) — Sections 9(b) and 32 of the Act set the deadline for LGUs to establish their own digital portal or adopt DICT's eLGU system at **one (1) year from the effectivity of the Act itself, not from its Implementing Rules and Regulations.** That places the deadline at approximately **late September 2026**. Separately, the Anti-Red Tape Authority has set **November 1, 2026** as the deadline for LGUs to have online business-permit processing (eBOSS) operational; as of December 31, 2025, only 127 of 1,642 LGUs nationwide were compliant. Both dates should anchor Phase 0/Phase 1 planning discussions, with the September 2026 date taking priority since it comes first.

## Chief Information Officer

- **Legal basis:** RA 12254 (E-Governance Act of 2025) requires the City Government to designate a CIO responsible for leading digital transformation, managing ICT systems, and ensuring compliance with national ICT standards.
- **Proposed relationship to this platform:** The CIO is proposed as the executive sponsor and chair of the IT Steering Committee below, giving this platform's governance structure a single accountable official rather than a committee without a named head.
- **Proposed responsibilities specific to this platform:** Ensures the platform's architecture and Roadmap remain aligned with DICT's E-Government Master Plan as it is published and updated; represents the platform in any DICT or MITHI-related reporting; formally owns the decision to pursue an Information Systems Strategic Plan (ISSP) submission, if the City chooses to pursue one.
- *Caveat: whether an existing official is designated CIO, or a new position is created, is a City Government appointment decision — not an engineering decision. This section identifies the requirement and a proposed relationship to platform governance, not a specific appointee.*

---

## Proposed Governance Bodies

### IT Steering Committee

- **Composition:** Chaired by the City's designated Chief Information Officer, with the City Administrator's Department, City Information and Communications Technology Department (ICTD), City Legal Department, City Budget Department, and rotating Department Heads from departments active in the current Roadmap phase.
- **Responsibilities:** Approves phase-gate transitions defined in the Project Roadmap, approves budget requests tied to the (forthcoming) Budget Framework, resolves cross-department disputes that a single department cannot settle on its own, and confirms the platform's continued alignment with the national E-Government Master Plan.
- **Cadence:** Meets at minimum at every Roadmap phase gate, and on-demand for urgent escalations.

### Technical Architecture Review Board

- **Composition:** Led by the City Information and Communications Technology Department (ICTD), with technical leads from active service domains.
- **Responsibilities:** Performs the "architecture review" already required throughout the Development Workflow and Branching Strategy sections for changes affecting shared contracts, identity, security, or data governance. Recommends, but does not independently approve, budget-impacting decisions — those escalate to the IT Steering Committee.
- **Cadence:** Continuous, tied to the pull request and planning cadence defined in the Development Workflow.

---

## Escalation Path

A module or service change follows this path when it cannot be resolved at the working level:

1. **Module or Service Owner** — first point of resolution for day-to-day issues, per the ownership model in the Repository Structure.
2. **Technical Architecture Review Board** — for issues involving shared contracts, security, identity, or data governance.
3. **IT Steering Committee** — for issues involving budget, cross-department conflict, or Roadmap phase-gate decisions.
4. **Office of the City Mayor / Sangguniang Panlungsod** — reserved for matters requiring executive or legislative action, such as ordinance changes or budget appropriation beyond the Steering Committee's authority.

## Dependency Note: CIO and Data Protection Officer Appointments

The CIO designation (above) and the Data Protection Officer appointment (Legal and Regulatory Compliance, Data Privacy) are independent statutory obligations under RA 12254 and RA 10173 respectively, but this platform's governance model has a single point of dependency on both: the IT Steering Committee cannot be meaningfully chaired until a CIO is named, and the Phase 0 NPC-registration compliance item cannot proceed until a DPO is designated. A delay in either appointment stalls two separate workstreams at once.

This is not a reason to delay Phase 0 planning. It is a reason for the City Administrator's Department to treat both appointments as shared upstream dependencies with an interim-designee fallback (e.g., an acting CIO or DPO drawn from ICTD or the City Legal Department) if a permanent appointment is not finalized before Phase 0 governance activities are scheduled to begin.

## National Government Integration

One Mandaluyong Super App complements—not replaces—national digital government initiatives.

> ⚠️ **Federation Decision — Proposed Direction, Pending IT Steering Committee Confirmation**
> This is no longer a "potential future integration" question. The national eGovPH super app was originally launched in January 2024 and had its major national "Super App" relaunch on July 18, 2025, led by the Office of the President — it already covers permits, clearances, civil registry documents, identity verification, and payments — the same ground much of the Citizen Experience Layer was originally scoped to cover independently. Republic Act No. 12254 §9(a) requires LGUs providing frontline services to file an application for integration with DICT, and the Anti-Red Tape Authority's Central Business Portal (CBP) mandate under RA 11032 requires business-permit systems to integrate with DICT/ARTA's national infrastructure — with a November 1, 2026 compliance deadline. Building a fully independent citizen-facing layer without resolving this is now a decision with statutory and scheduling consequences, not a Phase 3 "maybe."
>
> **Proposed direction, module by module:**
> - **Business Registration and Permit Services** — federate through DICT's free `eNGU`/eBPLS system and the ARTA Central Business Portal rather than building an independent permitting engine. This is the module under the nearest deadline (November 1, 2026). *(Terminology note: `eNGU` refers to DICT's overarching next-generation LGU platform; `eBPLS`, the electronic Business Permit and Licensing System, is the specific business-permit module within it that satisfies the ARTA eBOSS integration requirement — these are not competing systems.)*
> - **Citizen identity verification** — federate through PhilSys/eGovDX-style credential exchange rather than building a parallel identity-verification system, while the platform's own Identity and Access Management (Security Architecture, Technology Stack) continues to govern authorization and RBAC within Mandaluyong's own operational systems.
> - **Payments** — federate through the national Government Digital Payment Systems framework (RA 12254 §9(c)) where available, rather than building independent payment rails as the primary channel.
> - **Government Operations Layer, Enterprise Intelligence Layer, Local Legislation and Ordinance Knowledge Base, barangay-level coordination, case management, and internal departmental workflows** — remain unique to Mandaluyong. Nothing at the national level replicates these, and they are the platform's actual long-term value, independent of the federation decision above.
>
> This reframes the platform's primary positioning toward the Government Operations and Enterprise Intelligence layers, with the Citizen Experience Layer built to federate national transactions rather than compete with them. This direction should be formally confirmed by the IT Steering Committee once constituted, given its budget and scope implications for Phase 1.

Where appropriate and officially supported, the platform is designed to integrate with national systems to provide a seamless citizen experience while maintaining alignment with national standards.

Integrations under this federation model include:

- **eLGU** — the DICT-provided system Mandaluyong must adopt if it does not stand up its own compliant portal by the RA 12254 deadline (approximately late September 2026).
- **eGovDX** for secure interoperability and information exchange with national registries.
- **eGovPH** as the national citizen-facing entry point for services the City chooses to federate rather than duplicate, per the decision above.
- **DICT's `eNGU`/eBPLS and the ARTA Central Business Portal** — see the Business Registration and Permit Services federation decision above.
- Other authorized national government platforms and services that enhance local public service delivery while complying with applicable laws, standards, and governance requirements.

# 13. Three-Layer Architecture

One Mandaluyong Super App adopts a three-layer architecture that separates citizen experience, government operations, and enterprise intelligence into distinct yet integrated layers.

This architecture promotes modularity, scalability, maintainability, interoperability, and continuous evolution while ensuring that each layer has a clear responsibility within the City's digital ecosystem.

Rather than organizing the platform around individual government departments, the architecture is capability-driven, allowing multiple departments and offices to collaborate seamlessly in delivering public services.

Each layer complements the others while remaining independently maintainable and extensible.

---

## Layer 1 — Citizen Experience

The Citizen Experience Layer serves as the primary interface between the City Government of Mandaluyong and the public.

It provides a unified, accessible, and user-friendly digital experience that enables residents, businesses, visitors, and other stakeholders to access local government services through a single platform.

Typical capabilities include:

- Unified Citizen Account
- Digital Citizen Profile
- Personalized Dashboard
- Barangay Services
- Business Services
- Local Tax Services
- Online Payments
- Appointment Scheduling
- Digital Documents
- Complaints and Feedback
- Announcements and Emergency Alerts
- AI-Assisted Citizen Support
- Notifications
- Service Tracking

The objective of this layer is to simplify interactions with the City Government while improving accessibility, transparency, convenience, and citizen satisfaction.

---

## Layer 2 — City Government Operations

The City Government Operations Layer supports the internal functions of the Local Government Unit (LGU).

It provides integrated operational capabilities that enable departments, offices, and personnel to collaborate efficiently while maintaining their respective authorities, responsibilities, and governance requirements.

Typical capabilities include:

- Department Operations Portal
- Digital Workflow Management
- Case Management
- Document and Records Management
- Executive Dashboards
- Reporting and Analytics
- Performance Monitoring
- Budget Monitoring
- Human Resource Management
- Procurement Management
- Asset Management
- Engineering Repositories
- Knowledge Management
- Approval Workflows
- Interdepartmental Collaboration

Rather than creating isolated departmental systems, this layer enables shared capabilities that reduce duplication, improve operational efficiency, and strengthen organizational collaboration.

---

## Layer 3 — AI-Assisted Enterprise Intelligence

The AI-Assisted Enterprise Intelligence Layer serves as the intelligence capability that supports the planning, engineering, governance, operation, and continuous improvement of the entire digital ecosystem.

This layer is not a citizen-facing application.

## Relationship Between the Layers

The three layers operate together as a unified ecosystem.

- The **Citizen Experience Layer** delivers digital services to residents and other stakeholders.
- The **City Government Operations Layer** manages internal government processes, workflows, and service delivery.
- The **AI-Assisted Enterprise Intelligence Layer** continuously supports planning, engineering, governance, analytics, and organizational learning across the platform.

Each layer has a distinct responsibility while sharing common enterprise services, data, security, governance, and technology infrastructure.

This separation of concerns enables the platform to evolve over time without disrupting existing services while supporting future innovation, interoperability, and digital transformation initiatives.

# 16. Solution-Oriented Engineering

## Overview

One Mandaluyong Super App adopts a solution-oriented engineering approach to ensure that technology investments consistently address real public service needs, improve government operations, and create measurable value for the City Government of Mandaluyong and its stakeholders.

Rather than building technology for its own sake, every initiative begins with a clear understanding of the problem, its underlying causes, and the desired outcomes. Technology is therefore treated as an enabler of better public service—not as the objective itself.

This engineering philosophy guides the planning, design, implementation, operation, governance, and continuous improvement of the platform.

---

## Engineering Philosophy

Solution-oriented engineering focuses on solving problems before implementing technology.

Instead of asking:

> **"What feature should we build?"**

The engineering team begins by asking:

- What problem are we trying to solve?
- What is the root cause of the problem?
- Who is affected by the problem?
- Which city capability requires improvement?
- Can an existing capability be reused?
- Can departments collaborate instead of creating duplicate solutions?
- Will the proposed solution improve public service delivery?
- Is the solution sustainable, secure, and scalable?
- Can the solution evolve over time through continuous improvement?

Only after these questions have been addressed should implementation begin.

---

## Engineering Decision Framework

Every engineering decision should be evaluated using the following framework:

### 1. Understand the Problem

Clearly define the operational or citizen problem before considering potential solutions.

### 2. Analyze the Root Cause

Identify the underlying causes rather than treating only the symptoms.

### 3. Define the Desired Outcomes

Establish measurable objectives that represent successful problem resolution.

### 4. Assess Existing Capabilities

Evaluate whether current systems, services, or processes can be enhanced or reused before introducing new components.

### 5. Design the Solution

Develop solutions that are:

- Citizen-centered
- Capability-driven
- Secure
- Interoperable
- Maintainable
- Scalable
- Sustainable

### 6. Validate the Solution

Assess the proposed solution against:

- Engineering standards
- Enterprise architecture
- Security requirements
- Data governance
- Operational feasibility
- Long-term maintainability

### 7. Implement Incrementally

Deliver solutions through manageable iterations that reduce risk, encourage feedback, and support continuous improvement.

### 8. Measure Outcomes

Evaluate whether the implemented solution achieves the intended objectives using operational metrics, citizen feedback, and performance indicators.

### 9. Continuously Improve

Use evidence, analytics, organizational learning, and stakeholder feedback to refine and evolve the solution over time.

## Records Retention Schedule

Government records generated by this platform — citizen transaction logs, permits and certificates issued, payment records, audit logs, and Privacy Impact Assessment documentation — are government records subject to Republic Act No. 9470 (the National Archives of the Philippines Act of 2007), not merely technical data subject only to this platform's own storage policy.

> ⚠️ **Requirement Identified — Specific Periods Not Yet Set**
> This subsection names the legal requirement and the categories of platform-generated records that will need defined retention periods. It does not invent specific retention periods (e.g., "5 years," "10 years") for any record category. Under RA 9470, retention and disposition periods are set through a **Records Disposition Schedule**, which must be reviewed and approved by the **National Archives of the Philippines (NAP)** — not determined unilaterally by an engineering team or by City ICTD. Actual periods per record category must be confirmed with NAP before Phase 1 (Limited Pilot) generates real citizen records.

### Record Categories Requiring a Retention Period

- Citizen transaction logs (e.g. permit applications, service requests)
- Permits, certificates, and other documents issued through the platform
- Payment and financial transaction records
- Audit trails and system activity logs (Auditability and Traceability, above)
- Privacy Impact Assessment documentation (Legal and Regulatory Compliance, Compliance Checklist)
- Data-sharing agreements and interdepartmental data exchange records

### What This Platform Commits to, Independent of NAP-Set Periods

- No platform module deletes or purges citizen records based on an internally invented retention period. Deletion and archival logic waits on the NAP-approved Records Disposition Schedule.
- Until a Records Disposition Schedule is approved, records are retained by default rather than deleted, consistent with the precautionary approach already used elsewhere in this document for undetermined legal requirements.
- The Records Disposition Schedule, once approved, is implemented as an explicit, auditable data lifecycle policy — not left to individual module owners to interpret independently.

### Relationship with Other Sections

- Extends the Auditability and Traceability practices above with an actual lifecycle end-point for retained records.
- Feeds the Compliance Checklist by Phase in Legal and Regulatory Compliance, since a confirmed Records Disposition Schedule is needed before Phase 1 pilot data accumulates without a defined retention plan.
- Should be reflected as a Governance KPI in the Monitoring and Evaluation Framework once a schedule is approved (e.g., percentage of record categories with a confirmed disposition schedule).

This subsection should be finalized once the City Government of Mandaluyong initiates the Records Disposition Schedule process with the National Archives of the Philippines, ideally before Phase 1 (Limited Pilot) begins.

## Local Government Authority

- **Republic Act No. 7160, the Local Government Code of 1991** — establishes the legal authority of the City Government of Mandaluyong to deliver local services, collect local taxes and fees, and enter into agreements necessary for local governance, including digital service delivery. Platform modules that touch local taxation, business permitting, or barangay coordination trace their authority to this Code and to relevant Mandaluyong City ordinances.

## Data Privacy

- **Republic Act No. 10173, the Data Privacy Act of 2012**, and its Implementing Rules and Regulations — governs the collection, processing, storage, and disclosure of citizen personal data across all platform modules, particularly the Unified Citizen Account, health-related services, and any AI-Assisted Enterprise Intelligence processing of personal data.
- Compliance activities include registration of data processing systems with the National Privacy Commission (NPC), a Privacy Impact Assessment (PIA) prior to launch of any module processing personal data, appointment of a Data Protection Officer, breach notification procedures, and data-sharing agreements for any interdepartmental or national-government data exchange.
- The platform's Data Architecture and Security Architecture sections implement the technical and organizational measures required under this Act; this section establishes the legal basis for those measures.

## Ease of Doing Business and Efficient Government Service Delivery

- **Republic Act No. 11032, the Ease of Doing Business and Efficient Government Service Delivery Act of 2018**, and its Implementing Rules and Regulations — sets mandatory maximum processing times for government transactions (generally 3 working days for simple transactions, 7 working days for complex transactions, and 20 working days for highly technical transactions, subject to permissible extensions), prohibits multiple signatories beyond what is allowed, and requires a published Citizen's Charter.
- Business Registration and Permit Services, Local Tax and Revenue Services, and Digital Documents and Certifications modules are directly governed by this Act's processing-time ceilings. Service Tracking and Status Monitoring capabilities are designed, in part, to demonstrate compliance with these timelines.
- The platform's Citizen's Charter obligations under this Act should be published and kept current as modules go live.

## Government Procurement

- **Republic Act No. 12009, the New Government Procurement Act (NGPA)**, which took effect in August 2024 and repealed the former Government Procurement Reform Act (Republic Act No. 9184) — governs how the City Government of Mandaluyong procures the goods, infrastructure, and consulting services (including software development and system integration) needed to build the platform.
- A three-year transitory period applies from the NGPA's effectivity, during which prior procurement rules under Republic Act No. 9184 continue to apply to procurement activities not yet covered by the NGPA's Implementing Rules and Regulations; the applicable regime for any specific procurement should be confirmed with the City's Bids and Awards Committee (BAC) and Legal Office at the time that procurement is initiated, as transitional coverage may change.
- Any Terms of Reference, Request for Proposal, or bidding document produced for this platform is a separate deliverable from this engineering blueprint and must be drafted and reviewed against the NGPA and its current Implementing Rules and Regulations, not against the repealed Republic Act No. 9184.

## Government Digital Transformation Mandate

- **Republic Act No. 12254, the E-Governance Act**, signed September 5, 2025 — designates DICT as lead implementing body for government digital transformation across all LGUs, mandates a Chief Information Officer (see IT Governance Structure), and requires LGUs to establish their own digital service portal or adopt DICT's eLGU system within one year of the Act's effectivity (approximately late September 2026 — see the corrected timeline in IT Governance Structure for why this is measured from the Act itself, and not its IRR).
- Separately, the Anti-Red Tape Authority has set November 1, 2026 as the deadline for LGUs to have online business-permit processing (eBOSS) operational, integrated with DICT's free `eNGU` system and the Bureau of Fire Protection. This directly governs the Business Registration and Permit Services module.
- The Act also sets minimum standards for government websites and digital portals, including an e-Bulletin Board, API-accessible public information, a public staff directory, Philippine Web Accessibility Policy compliance, and a real-time citizen feedback mechanism with results publicly aggregated and reported quarterly. These should be reflected in the Citizen Engagement capabilities under Key Features and System Modules, not treated as satisfied by general accessibility commitments alone.
- **The Philippine Government Interoperability Framework (PGIF)**, established under RA 12254's IRR, sets the specific data-exchange and once-only-principle standards that this platform's API-first integration approach must conform to. The platform's shared APIs, event contracts, and cross-department data exchange (Data Architecture, Technology Stack) are the technical mechanisms through which PGIF compliance is achieved; they are not a separate, unrelated engineering choice from the PGIF obligation. PGIF conformance should be confirmed with DICT before any interdepartmental or national-government data exchange goes live, consistent with the Compliance Checklist by Phase, below.

## Records Management

- **Republic Act No. 9470, the National Archives of the Philippines Act of 2007** — government records generated by this platform (citizen transaction logs, permits, certificates, payment records, audit trails, Privacy Impact Assessment documentation) are subject to this Act, not merely to the platform's own storage policy. The full requirement, including the Records Disposition Schedule process with the National Archives of the Philippines, is detailed in Data Architecture's Records Retention Schedule subsection; it is cited here because this section, not Data Architecture, is the canonical legal reference the City Legal Department reviews.

## Digital Identity

- **Republic Act No. 11055, the Philippine Identification System Act (PhilSys Act)**, and its Implementing Rules and Regulations — governs how the platform verifies citizen identity. Consistent with the federation decision in Key Features (National Government Integration), identity verification is federated through PhilSys rather than built as an independent identity-verification system; the platform's own Identity and Access Management (Security Architecture) continues to govern authentication and authorization for Mandaluyong's own operational systems.

## Electronic Transactions

- **Republic Act No. 8792, the Electronic Commerce Act of 2000** — governs the legal validity of electronic documents, electronic signatures, and electronic transactions. This is the legal basis for treating a digitally issued permit, certificate, or receipt as equivalent to its paper counterpart; it applies directly to Digital Documents and Certifications and to any module that issues an official document electronically.

## Accessibility and Inclusion

- **Republic Act No. 7277, the Magna Carta for Persons with Disabilities**, as amended by Republic Act No. 9442 and Republic Act No. 10754 — informs the accessibility commitments already reflected in the platform's Coding Standards (WCAG-aligned checks for citizen-facing interfaces) and the Philippine Web Accessibility Policy compliance obligation named under Government Digital Transformation Mandate, above.

## Cybersecurity and Critical Information Infrastructure

- **Republic Act No. 10175, the Cybercrime Prevention Act of 2012** — governs obligations relating to critical information infrastructure protection and incident reporting to relevant national cybersecurity authorities; informs the platform's Security Architecture, including its incident reporting and vulnerability management processes.

## Transparency and Public Access to Information

- **Executive Order No. 2, s. 2016 (Freedom of Information — Executive Branch)** — governs public access to government records and information. Relevant to the platform's public-facing data (e.g., ordinance knowledge base, public dashboards) and to how citizen requests for government records are handled, distinct from a citizen's own personal data rights under the Data Privacy Act, above.

# 20. Technology Stack

One Mandaluyong Super App is built on an open-standards, cloud-native, government-grade technology foundation designed to support long-term maintainability, interoperability, and continuous evolution rather than short-term convenience.

The technology stack is selected to serve the platform's Three-Layer Architecture — Citizen Experience, Government Operations, and AI-Assisted Enterprise Intelligence — while remaining domain-oriented and modular rather than a single monolithic system. Each component is chosen for its ability to support a 10–20 year public-sector platform: proven enterprise reliability, strong security posture, active open-source governance, and clear upgrade paths.

---

## Technology Stack Principles

The selection of technologies is guided by the following principles:

- Open Standards
- Cloud-Native Design
- API-First Integration
- Domain-Oriented Modularity
- Vendor Neutrality
- Security by Design
- Long-Term Maintainability
- Scalability
- Interoperability
- Auditability and Governance
- Continuous Improvement

These principles ensure that technology choices strengthen, rather than constrain, the platform's ability to evolve alongside the City's needs.

---

## Citizen Experience Layer Technologies

### Web Platform

- Next.js with TypeScript for the citizen portal and public-facing web experiences
- Tailwind CSS and an accessible component library for a consistent design system
- Server-side rendering for performance, accessibility, and search visibility

### Mobile Platform

- Flutter for a single, unified codebase across Android and iOS
- Progressive Web App (PWA) support for lightweight citizen interactions
- Native platform wrappers used only where deep device integration is required

## Government Operations Layer Technologies

### Core Application Services

- Java 21 with Spring Boot 3 for core transactional and domain services
- Domain-oriented service boundaries for each department rather than a single shared backend
- REST APIs governed by OpenAPI contracts
- Event-driven integration for asynchronous, cross-department workflows

### Identity and Access Management

- Keycloak for identity, authentication, and access management using open standards (OIDC/SAML)
- Centralized enforcement of Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) across all services

### API Management

- Kong as the enterprise API Gateway
- Centralized rate limiting, versioning, authentication enforcement, and audit logging for all internal and external APIs

### Event Streaming and Integration

- Apache Kafka as the platform's event backbone
- Enables durable, scalable, and auditable communication between departments and services

---

## AI-Assisted Enterprise Intelligence Layer Technologies

- Python with FastAPI for AI, analytics, and document-processing services
- A hybrid AI approach combining self-hosted open-source models for internal use with managed models for specific, approved needs
- Vector search capability through pgvector and OpenSearch to support semantic and knowledge retrieval
- Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) pipelines for governed, policy-grounded knowledge access
- Human-in-the-loop review embedded into all high-impact AI-assisted recommendations

This approach ensures artificial intelligence remains a governed engineering and decision-support capability rather than a vendor-dependent black box.

---

## Data and Search Technologies

### System of Record

- PostgreSQL as the platform's primary transactional database and system of record
- **Isolation boundary: schema-per-service, minimum, one schema per bounded context** (e.g. `tax_and_revenue`, `business_registration`, `case_management`), each with its own credentials and access grants — not a single shared schema. Separate database instances per bounded context are the stronger alternative and may be adopted for services with distinct scaling, compliance, or availability needs, but schema-per-service is the minimum acceptable isolation for Phase 0.
- **Master Data Management (MDM) is a read-pattern, not a shared-write pattern**: each service owns and writes only to its own schema; other services consume shared reference data (citizen, business, address, organization master data) through the MDM module's published read APIs or synchronized read replicas, never through direct cross-schema writes. This keeps the "modular architecture" principle from Repository Structure enforced at the database layer, not just in code organization.
- PostGIS extension for geographic and location-based information
- pgvector extension to support AI and semantic search use cases
- Partitioning strategies for large-scale transactional tables

### Search and Knowledge Retrieval

- OpenSearch for full-text and hybrid search across citizen services, records, and institutional knowledge repositories

### Document and Object Storage

- Managed object storage for documents, attachments, and unstructured records, separate from transactional data

### Analytics Expansion Path

- Structured, governed exports to a dedicated data warehouse (or a solution such as ClickHouse) as analytics and reporting needs grow
- ETL/ELT pipelines with data quality checks, lineage, and ownership tagging

A single database is intentionally not used for every workload; transactional processing, document storage, search, and analytics are treated as distinct, purpose-fit capabilities.

---

## Infrastructure and Cloud Technologies

- Kubernetes as the container orchestration foundation for all stateless and stateful workloads
- Azure Government as the preferred cloud environment, with AWS GovCloud as a viable alternative depending on procurement direction
- Managed database, object storage, secrets management, and private networking services
- Web Application Firewall (WAF) and API gateway protection at the network edge

### Infrastructure as Code

- Terraform for provisioning cloud infrastructure
- Helm, and Kustomize where needed, for Kubernetes application configuration
- Environment promotion pipelines across development, testing, staging, and production

---

## DevOps and Delivery

- GitLab CI/CD, or GitHub Enterprise where procurement direction favors it, with self-hosted runners
- Trunk-based development with branch protection and mandatory review
- Container image scanning, policy-as-code, and automated security checks
- Feature flags, progressive delivery, canary deployments, and automated rollback

---

## Observability and Monitoring

- OpenTelemetry for consistent instrumentation across all services
- Prometheus for metrics collection
- Grafana for dashboards and operational visualization
- Loki for centralized log aggregation
- Tempo for distributed tracing

Together, these tools give engineering teams and government leaders continuous visibility into service health, API performance, workflow failures, citizen-facing incidents, and audit events.

---

## Security Technologies

- HashiCorp Vault or Azure Key Vault for centralized secrets management
- Static, dynamic, and software composition analysis (SAST/DAST/SCA) integrated into the development lifecycle
- Container image scanning and Software Bill of Materials (SBOM) generation
- Zero Trust principles applied through service-to-service authentication and mutual TLS where practical

These technologies operationalize the controls defined in the Security Architecture.

## Technologies Deliberately Avoided

To protect the platform's long-term governability, certain technology patterns are intentionally avoided for the platform core:

- Firebase or Supabase as the primary system of record for a city-wide platform
- A custom-built authentication system in place of a standards-based identity provider
- A single monolithic backend serving all departments without domain boundaries
- A purely serverless architecture for the entire platform
- NoSQL databases as the primary system of record for transactional government workflows
- Heavy reliance on low-code tools for core enterprise services

These exclusions favor long-term maintainability, governance, portability, and auditability over short-term development convenience.

- The **Citizen Experience Layer** is delivered through Next.js, TypeScript, and Flutter, prioritizing accessibility, performance, and a consistent citizen-facing experience.
- The **Government Operations Layer** is delivered through Java, Spring Boot, Keycloak, Kong, and Kafka, prioritizing transactional integrity, security, and cross-department interoperability.
- The **AI-Assisted Enterprise Intelligence Layer** is delivered through Python, FastAPI, and governed retrieval pipelines, prioritizing evidence-based support under human oversight.

Technology choices are therefore not independent decisions but deliberate extensions of the platform's architectural and governance model.

---

By adopting this technology stack, the City Government of Mandaluyong aims to achieve:

The Technology Stack establishes the technical foundation that enables the One Mandaluyong Super App to be built, operated, secured, and continuously evolved as a resilient, citizen-centered digital government platform.

# 22. Development Workflow

One Mandaluyong Super App follows a disciplined, governed development workflow designed for a long-lived, multi-department government platform rather than a short-lived project. The workflow emphasizes continuous integration, mandatory review, automated quality gates, and architecture governance, so that the platform can scale to many contributors and departments without losing consistency or accountability.

This section describes how work moves from an idea to a running service in production. Detailed source-code conventions are defined in Coding Standards, and the branch model itself is defined in Branching Strategy; this section focuses on the process that connects them.

---

## Development Workflow Principles

- Trunk-Based Development
- Small, Frequent, Reviewable Changes
- Mandatory Peer Review
- Architecture Review for Significant Changes
- Automated Quality and Security Gates
- API Contract Testing Before Integration
- Environment-Based Promotion
- Traceability from Requirement to Deployment
- Continuous Feedback and Improvement

These principles keep day-to-day engineering activity aligned with the platform's broader governance and security architecture.

---

## Workflow Stages

### 1. Planning and Design

- Work is defined as tracked issues or tickets linked to a module, department, or capability.
- Changes affecting shared platform services, data contracts, or cross-department integration are flagged for architecture review before implementation begins.
- API and event contract changes are drafted and reviewed before code is written, consistent with the platform's API-first approach.

### 2. Implementation

- Development happens on short-lived branches created directly from the trunk, following the conventions defined in Branching Strategy.
- Code is written according to the conventions defined in Coding Standards, including secure coding practices and consistent module boundaries.
- Contributors keep changes small and scoped to a single module wherever possible, to simplify review and reduce integration risk.

### 3. Code Review

- Every change requires mandatory peer review before merging, regardless of contributor seniority.
- Reviewers verify correctness, security, adherence to module boundaries, and alignment with existing architectural decisions.
- Changes that introduce new dependencies, new services, or modify shared contracts require an additional architecture review.

### 4. Automated Quality and Security Gates

- Continuous integration pipelines run automated tests, static analysis (SAST), dependency scanning (SCA), and container image scanning on every change.
- API contract tests validate that services continue to honor their published OpenAPI and event contracts.
- Builds that fail any quality or security gate are blocked from merging or promotion.

### 5. Integration and Continuous Delivery

- Approved changes are merged into the trunk and built into immutable, versioned artifacts.
- Continuous delivery pipelines package and prepare these artifacts for progressive rollout, using feature flags where appropriate to decouple deployment from release.

### 6. Environment Promotion

- Changes are promoted sequentially through development, testing, staging, and production environments.
- Each promotion step requires passing environment-specific validation and, for production, an explicit deployment approval.
- Promotion decisions and approvals are logged to preserve traceability and support audits.

### 7. Post-Deployment Feedback

- Observability data, incident reports, and citizen or department feedback feed back into planning for subsequent iterations.
- Recurring issues, architectural friction, or lessons learned are captured in the Knowledge Management and Repository Management modules for institutional reuse.

---

## Architecture Governance in the Workflow

Not every change carries the same risk, so the workflow applies architecture review proportionally:

- Routine changes within a single module's established boundaries follow standard peer review.
- Changes that introduce new shared contracts, new external integrations, or new infrastructure require architecture review before implementation.
- Changes affecting identity, security controls, or data governance require sign-off consistent with the Security Architecture and Data Architecture.

This graduated approach avoids slowing down routine work while still protecting the platform's long-term architectural integrity.

---

## Relationship with Other Engineering Practices

- **Coding Standards** define how code is written; this workflow defines how that code moves safely into production.
- **Branching Strategy** defines the specific branch model used within the "Implementation" and "Integration" stages described above.
- **Security Architecture** and **Data Architecture** define the controls that the automated quality gates in this workflow enforce.
- **AI-Assisted Enterprise Intelligence** may support this workflow through automated code review assistance, standards validation, and governance intelligence, always under human review and final approval.

---

## Expected Outcomes

By following a disciplined, governed development workflow, the City Government of Mandaluyong aims to achieve:

- Faster, safer delivery of new capabilities
- Consistent quality and security across all departments and modules
- Reduced integration risk through small, frequent changes
- Stronger architectural integrity as the platform scales
- Clear traceability from requirement to production deployment
- A repeatable process that remains effective as contributor and department count grows

The Development Workflow ensures that One Mandaluyong Super App is not only well-architected on paper, but consistently and safely built, reviewed, and delivered in practice.

# 23. Coding Standards

Coding standards for One Mandaluyong Super App exist to ensure that code produced by different departments, teams, and contributors over many years remains consistent, secure, and maintainable. Standards are treated as a shared engineering asset, not a matter of individual preference.

---

## Coding Standards Principles

- Consistency Across Modules and Teams
- Readability Over Cleverness
- Security by Default
- Testability by Design
- Explicit, Documented API Contracts
- Automated Enforcement Over Manual Policing
- Language-Appropriate Idioms
- Consistent Naming Aligned with Domain Boundaries

---

## Backend Standards (Java 21 + Spring Boot 3)

- Follow standard Java and Spring Boot conventions for package structure, dependency injection, and layering (controller, service, repository).
- Each service exposes its contract through OpenAPI; contracts are written and reviewed before implementation.
- Business logic is kept in service layers, not controllers, and is covered by unit and integration tests.
- Input validation, output encoding, and centralized exception handling are mandatory, consistent with the Security Architecture.
- Static analysis (SAST) and dependency scanning (SCA) run automatically on every change.

## AI and Analytics Standards (Python 3.12 + FastAPI)

- Follow PEP 8 and type-hinted function signatures throughout.
- AI/ML pipelines separate data ingestion, model inference, and business logic into distinct, testable components.
- All AI-assisted recommendations are logged with enough context to support human review and audit, consistent with AI-Assisted Enterprise Intelligence principles.
- Prompt templates, RAG retrieval logic, and model configuration are version-controlled, not hardcoded inline.

## Frontend Standards (Next.js + TypeScript)

- Strict TypeScript typing is enforced; use of `any` is discouraged and requires justification in review.
- Components follow the shared design system in `libs/shared-ui` rather than introducing one-off styles.
- Accessibility (WCAG-aligned) checks are part of the standard review checklist for citizen-facing interfaces.
- Data fetching uses documented API contracts only; no direct, undocumented calls to backend services.

## Mobile Standards (Flutter)

- Follow standard Dart style conventions and the official Flutter linting rules.
- Shared UI components mirror the web design system wherever platform constraints allow.
- Offline handling and error states are explicitly designed for, given variable citizen connectivity conditions.

## General Standards Across All Languages

- Every module includes automated tests appropriate to its risk level, with critical transactional logic held to the highest coverage expectations.
- Secrets, credentials, and configuration are never hardcoded; they are sourced from the platform's secrets management tooling.
- Every non-trivial change includes updated documentation in `docs/` or inline API documentation.
- Linting, formatting, and static analysis are enforced automatically in continuous integration, not left to manual review alone.

---

## Expected Outcomes

- Consistent, predictable code across departments and modules
- Lower onboarding cost for new contributors
- Reduced defect and vulnerability rates
- Easier long-term maintenance as teams and priorities change over time

# 24. Branching Strategy

One Mandaluyong Super App follows a trunk-based branching strategy, consistent with the Development Workflow, designed to keep the platform continuously integrable across many concurrent contributors and departments.

---

## Branching Principles

- A Single, Always-Deployable Trunk
- Short-Lived Feature Branches
- Small, Frequent Merges
- Protected Trunk and Release Branches
- Feature Flags Over Long-Lived Branches
- Traceable History Between Issues, Branches, and Releases

---

## Branch Model

### Trunk (`main`)

- Represents the current, always-integrable state of the platform.
- Protected: direct commits are disallowed; all changes enter through reviewed, approved pull requests.
- Must pass all automated quality and security gates before any merge is allowed.

### Feature Branches

- Created from the trunk for a single, scoped change (e.g., `feature/business-permit-renewal`, `fix/appointment-queue-bug`).
- Kept short-lived — typically merged back within days, not weeks — to minimize integration risk.
- Named consistently to indicate type (`feature/`, `fix/`, `chore/`, `hotfix/`) and the module or capability affected.

### Release Branches and Tags

- Production releases are marked with immutable version tags rather than long-lived release branches, wherever practical.
- Where a release branch is required for stabilization, it is short-lived and merged back or retired promptly after release.

### Hotfix Branches

- Reserved for urgent production fixes.
- Follow an expedited but still mandatory review and testing path before merging into the trunk and being promoted through environments.

---

## Merge and Protection Rules

- Mandatory peer review before any merge into the trunk.
- Required automated checks: build, tests, API contract tests, SAST/SCA, container scanning.
- Architecture review required for changes affecting shared contracts, identity, security, or data governance.
- No direct pushes to protected branches under any circumstance, including for administrators.

---

## Relationship with the Development Workflow

This branching model implements the "Implementation" and "Integration" stages defined in the Development Workflow. Environment promotion, deployment approvals, and rollback procedures continue to apply after a change merges into the trunk.

---

## Expected Outcomes

- Minimal integration conflicts despite many concurrent contributors
- A trunk that remains deployable at all times
- Clear, auditable history connecting requirements, code changes, and releases
- Faster recovery from defects through short-lived, easily revertible branches

# 25. Project Roadmap

The Project Roadmap for One Mandaluyong Super App follows a phased, evidence-based approach. Rather than attempting a single citywide launch, the platform is introduced incrementally, with each phase validated before expanding scope, consistent with the platform's solution-oriented engineering philosophy.

---

## Roadmap Principles

- Incremental, Evidence-Based Expansion
- Governance and Security Established Before Scale
- Reuse Before Replacement of Existing Systems
- Continuous Learning Between Phases
- Departments Onboarded in Manageable Waves

---

## Phase 0 — Foundation and Governance

- Establish core platform capabilities: identity and access management, API gateway, base data architecture, and observability stack.
- Finalize governance structures, coding standards, branching strategy, and security baselines.
- Stand up the repository structure and CI/CD pipelines.
- No citizen-facing features are launched in this phase; the goal is a stable, governed foundation.

## Phase 1 — Limited Pilot

- Launch a small set of high-value citizen services (for example, one or two of: Business Permit Services, Appointment Management, Digital Documents) with a limited group of participating departments.
- Validate identity, payments, notifications, and core workflow modules under real operating conditions.
- Establish baseline metrics for performance, security incidents, and citizen satisfaction.

## Phase 2 — Controlled Expansion

- Onboard additional departments and modules based on pilot learnings.
- Expand Enterprise Platform Modules (Master Data Management, Enterprise Search, Enterprise Integration) to support growing cross-department needs.
- Introduce the AI-Assisted Enterprise Intelligence Layer for internal engineering and decision support, under full human review.

## Phase 3 — Citywide Scale-Up

- Extend the platform to remaining departments and citizen services.
- Deepen interoperability with barangays and, where appropriate, national government platforms.
- Mature analytics and reporting capabilities, including the analytics/warehouse expansion path described in the Technology Stack.

## Phase 4 — Continuous Evolution

- Treat the platform as a permanent digital foundation rather than a finished project.
- Continuously incorporate lessons learned, citizen feedback, new integrations, and emerging technologies.
- Periodically reassess architecture, security posture, and governance practices against evolving needs.

---

## Roadmap Governance

Progression between phases is a governance decision, not an automatic timeline. Each phase transition considers:

- Stability and security of the current phase
- Departmental and citizen feedback
- Operational readiness of receiving departments
- Data governance and interoperability maturity

---

## Expected Outcomes

- Reduced risk compared to a single large-scale launch
- Continuous validation of assumptions before scaling
- Sustainable pace of adoption across departments
- A platform that matures deliberately rather than being rushed into citywide use

- **Operational KPIs** — platform uptime, incident count and severity, average resolution time
- **Governance KPIs** — Compliance Checklist completion rate per phase gate, risk register review completion
- **Financial KPIs** — budget variance against the future Budget Framework, cost per transaction

---

## KPI Focus by Phase

### Phase 0 — Foundation and Governance

Citizen KPIs are not yet applicable, since no citizen-facing features launch in this phase. Focus is on governance and infrastructure readiness:

- Citywide adoption rate and satisfaction score, compared against the Phase 1 pilot baseline
- Accessibility compliance rate across all citizen-facing modules
- Cost per transaction, compared against the pre-digital baseline where available

### Phase 4 — Continuous Evolution

- Trend analysis across all prior KPI categories, reviewed at each ongoing phase gate
- Rate of new risks identified and closed
- Citizen feedback themes incorporated into the Roadmap's continuous improvement cycle

*Caveat: specific numeric targets — for example, a target uptime percentage or satisfaction score threshold — are intentionally not set here. # 27. Risk Management

> ⚠️ **Proposed Owners Shown Below — Confirm With the City Administrator's Department**
> Each risk below now lists a **proposed owning office**, based on that office's publicly published mandate on the City Government of Mandaluyong's official channels. These are starting assignments for Phase 0 governance setup, not confirmed designations — ownership is assigned by office/title (not by the name of whoever currently holds the post, so the assignment survives personnel changes) and must still be formally confirmed by the **City Administrator's Department** once Phase 0 begins.

One Mandaluyong Super App operates in an environment shaped not only by technical complexity but by the political, organizational, and procurement realities of a Philippine local government unit. This section identifies the primary categories of risk facing the platform and the corresponding mitigation approach, complementing the technical risk controls already defined in the Security Architecture.

Risk management is treated as a continuous discipline tied to the Project Roadmap's phase gates, not a one-time assessment performed at project kickoff.

---

## Risk Management Principles

- Risk Identification Before Commitment, Not After Failure
- Political and Organizational Risk Given Equal Weight to Technical Risk
- Mitigation Ownership Assigned, Not Left Implicit
- Risk Register Reviewed at Every Roadmap Phase Gate
- Transparency With City Leadership Over Risk Concealment

---

## Political and Administrative Risk

- **Change in city administration or leadership priorities.** A change in Mayor, City Administrator, or department heads can alter sponsorship, funding priority, or continuity of the platform.
  - *Mitigation:* Anchor the platform's legal basis and institutional value in the Local Government Code and this document's Legal and Regulatory Compliance section rather than in any single administration's priorities; maintain the platform as a City Government asset with documented institutional ownership, not a personality-driven initiative; prioritize the phased, evidence-based rollout in the Project Roadmap so that value is demonstrated incrementally rather than dependent on a single long-term commitment.
  - *Proposed Owner:* City Administrator's Department
- **Delayed or contested budget appropriation.** Annual budget cycles and Sanggunian approval processes may delay funding for later phases.
  - *Mitigation:* Align each roadmap phase to a funding request that can be justified independently, based on the Expected Outcomes already demonstrated by the prior phase.
  - *Proposed Owner:* City Budget Department

## Procurement Risk

- **Delay from transition between Republic Act No. 9184 and the New Government Procurement Act (RA 12009).** Uncertainty over which procurement rules apply during the transitory period may slow down bidding.
  - *Mitigation:* Confirm applicable procurement regime with the City's Bids and Awards Committee and Legal Office at the time each procurement is initiated, per the Legal and Regulatory Compliance section; avoid drafting Terms of Reference against superseded law.
  - *Proposed Owner:* Bids and Awards Committee (BAC), with the City Legal Department
- **Failed or contested bidding, or lack of qualified bidders for a government-grade technology stack.**
  - *Mitigation:* Scope Phase 0 and Phase 1 procurement narrowly enough to attract qualified local bidders; consider phased or lot-based procurement rather than a single citywide contract.
  - *Proposed Owner:* Bids and Awards Committee (BAC)

## Organizational and Employee Adoption Risk

- **Resistance from government employees toward digitized workflows**, arising from unfamiliarity, fear of role elimination, or disruption to established informal processes.
  - *Mitigation:* Involve department personnel in Phase 0 and Phase 1 design and pilot activities rather than presenting the platform as a finished mandate; provide training tied to each department's onboarding wave; frame the AI-Assisted Enterprise Intelligence Layer explicitly as augmenting, not replacing, staff, consistent with the Engineering Philosophy.
  - *Proposed Owner:* City Administrator's Department, with the City Information and Communications Technology Department (ICTD)
- **Loss of institutional knowledge during personnel transitions.**
  - *Mitigation:* Rely on the Repository-First Engineering and Institutional Knowledge Management principles already established in this document to ensure knowledge persists independently of individual staff.
  - *Proposed Owner:* City Information and Communications Technology Department (ICTD)

## Technical and Operational Risk

- **Server, network, or cloud provider downtime affecting citizen-facing services.**
  - *Mitigation:* Addressed primarily through the Infrastructure and Cloud Technologies and Observability sections of the Technology Stack; this Risk Register tracks the operational and communication response (e.g., a citizen-facing status page and defined incident communication protocol), which is an organizational responsibility rather than purely a technical one.
  - *Proposed Owner:* City Information and Communications Technology Department (ICTD)
- **Vendor or platform lock-in** from over-reliance on a single cloud provider or proprietary component.
  - *Mitigation:* Continue to apply the Vendor Neutrality and Open Standards principles already defined in the Technology Stack.
  - *Proposed Owner:* City Information and Communications Technology Department (ICTD)
- **Data migration risk** when integrating or replacing legacy departmental systems.
  - *Mitigation:* Apply the "Reuse Before Replacement" principle from the Engineering Philosophy; require a validated migration and rollback plan before any legacy system is retired.
  - *Proposed Owner:* City Information and Communications Technology Department (ICTD), with the owning department of the legacy system

## Reputational and Public Trust Risk

- **Citizen data breach or misuse**, which could undermine public trust in the platform and the City Government.
  - *Mitigation:* Enforced primarily through the Security Architecture and the Data Privacy compliance obligations in the Legal and Regulatory Compliance section; this register additionally requires a pre-approved public communication protocol for any confirmed breach, consistent with Data Privacy Act notification obligations.
  - *Proposed Owner:* Data Protection Office (under the City Legal Department), with the City Public Information Office for external communication
- **Perception of the platform as excluding citizens without smartphones or reliable internet access.**
  - *Mitigation:* Preserve non-digital and in-person service channels alongside the platform, consistent with the principle that the platform complements rather than replaces existing services.
  - *Proposed Owner:* City Public Information Office

---

## Risk Register Governance

- The risks identified in this section are reviewed and updated at every Project Roadmap phase gate.
- Each risk above lists a **proposed owner**, based on that office's publicly published mandate; these are starting assignments for Phase 0 governance setup, not final designations. The **City Administrator's Department** is responsible for formally confirming, reassigning, or splitting ownership once Phase 0 governance structures are established.
- Ownership is assigned to an office or role, not to the name of a current officeholder, so that assignments remain valid across personnel changes, consistent with the Institutional Knowledge Management principle.
- New risks identified during any phase are added to this register rather than handled informally.

---

## Expected Outcomes

- Risks are identified and owned before they materialize into incidents or project failure
- City leadership has visibility into non-technical risk, not only cybersecurity risk
- Mitigation responsibility is traceable to a specific owner rather than assumed
- The platform's risk posture evolves deliberately alongside the Project Roadmap

# 30. Contributing

Contributions to One Mandaluyong Super App are expected from multiple city departments, engineering personnel, and — where appropriate — vetted external partners. Contribution practices are designed to preserve quality, security, and architectural integrity across all contributors.

---

## Who Can Contribute

- City Government of Mandaluyong Information and Communications Technology (ICT) personnel
- Designated technical staff from participating departments
- Approved external contractors or partners engaged under appropriate agreements
- Future contributors onboarded as the platform expands to additional departments

---

## Contribution Process

1. **Propose the Change** — Open a tracked issue describing the problem, proposed change, and affected module(s).
2. **Architecture Check** — For changes affecting shared contracts, security, identity, or data governance, request architecture review before implementation.
3. **Implement** — Develop the change on a short-lived branch following the Branching Strategy and Coding Standards.
4. **Test** — Include automated tests appropriate to the change's risk level; ensure all quality and security gates pass.
5. **Submit for Review** — Open a pull request referencing the originating issue; mandatory peer review applies to every change.
6. **Address Feedback** — Respond to review comments and required changes promptly and completely.
7. **Merge and Promote** — Once approved and all checks pass, the change is merged into the trunk and promoted through environments per the Development Workflow.

---

## Contribution Expectations

- Follow the Coding Standards and Branching Strategy without exception.
- Document new modules, APIs, and significant decisions in `docs/` as they are introduced.
- Disclose any security concerns, data privacy implications, or known limitations of a contribution during review.
- Treat institutional knowledge as shared: capture lessons learned rather than keeping them informal or undocumented.

---

## Reporting Issues and Requesting Features

- Defects, security concerns, and enhancement requests are submitted as tracked issues, tagged to the relevant module and department.
- Security-related issues follow the platform's incident reporting and vulnerability management process defined in the Security Architecture, rather than being filed as ordinary public issues.

---

## Expected Outcomes
